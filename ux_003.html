World Design and Lore Integration

When developing the world for UX_003, we approached it with the mindset of a cinematic production team rather than simply creating a “stage.” The objective wasn’t just to host performances—it was to immerse attendees in a lore-driven environment that felt like a real location from the EVE narrative universe. The goal was to blur the line between digital performance and storytelling. We started with a conceptual design rooted in the EVE mythos: a decommissioned, subterranean bunker, built decades prior by Holorise Industries, hidden beneath the decaying surface of a world slowly forgetting its own history. This space was to feel like a forgotten node—an access point to something dormant. It had to feel functional, utilitarian, and outdated, but still haunted by memory. The physicality of the space would reflect the emotional themes of isolation, technological decay, and the longing for reconnection.

Blocking and Previsualization in Blender

The world was first blocked out in Blender, where we iterated quickly using reference boards and modular geometry. We used kitbash libraries of pipes, metal wall panels, access terminals, server racks, and military-grade flooring to assemble a believable industrial aesthetic (Later largely replaced by custom made assets). Lighting was simulated early using Blender’s CYCLES engine just to get a sense of placement and spatial mood, but we knew all final lighting would be done in Unity.

During this phase, we paid close attention to:

- Player scale in VR (ensuring that doorways, hallways, and ceilings were realistic for first-person perspective)

- Navigation flow—the world needed to guide attendees toward the stage naturally, without UI or markers

- Visual storytelling: placing props like overturned chairs, broken equipment, glowing server cores, faded warning signs, and overgrowth through vents—all elements that hinted at a larger collapse without spelling it out

Every asset we built had to serve both form and function—the camera team needed stable perches and paths, performers needed access points to the stage, and the audience needed freedom of movement without losing immersion.

Importing to Unity & VRC Compatibility

Once the core Blender scene was assembled, we exported the world as FBX with proper origin anchors, separated mesh hierarchies, and pivot-aligned sub-objects to allow for later prefab instancing and animation. From there, we transitioned to Unity, using the VRChat Creator Companion (VCC) to initialize a Udon-compatible world project. From the Unity side, we handled a large number of critical VRC constraints and setup tasks:

1. Colliders and Navigation Meshes
Every object was either baked into a static collider mesh (merged for performance) or left as a dynamic collider if interaction was required.

- We created a custom set of colliders specific to individual whitelists, ensuring it didn’t interfere with flying camera rigs or floating UI elements. One for artists, One for staff, One for attendees, and one for VIPS.

- Invisible walls were added with these whitelists to prevent users from getting stuck in dead corners or from breaking immersion by accessing unfinished areas, or accessing areas their whitelist should not access.

2. Lightmapping and Realtime Lighting The scene was divided into static and dynamic lighting layers. Static props used baked GI with light probes for ambient bounce, while dynamic elements (like screens, animations, and players) relied on mixed or real-time lighting.

- We used Baked Lighting with Shadowmask for most light sources to maintain real-time shadows on avatars without taxing performance. - We utilized VRSL stage lighting for VRC to integrate a fully functional Gridnode based DMX controllable lighting system into the world. All of the lighting was manually programmed individually per artist, per song.

- House lighting was built with scripted flickers, giving the illusion of an unstable power grid and enhancing tension, though this was not used in the final version of the world as it was causing issues for individuals with varied graphics settings.

3. Shader Optimization Custom shaders were developed for key surfaces using Poiyomi and VRChat compatible shader variants where necessary.

- We used Parallax Occlusion Mapping for wall details, which gave the illusion of depth while using flat geometry.

- We developed our own in-house custom screen shader. This shader made the screen look perfectly crystal clear at a distance, but as you got closer, it would slowly break apart into individual Red, Green and Blue pixels, so it would look like a legitimate LED wall when standing right next to it.
